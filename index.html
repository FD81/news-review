L'accord de cessez-le-feu du 17 avril est très loin de rassurer les Libanais, alors que les autorités ont accepté le principe de négociations directes avec Israël dans l'objectif d'une accalmie durable. Entre une « ligne jaune » israélienne établissant une nouvelle zone d'occupation dans le sud du pays et les « lignes rouges » du Hezbollah, qui refuse un retour à l'équation imposée par la trêve précédente de novembre 2024, la marge de manoeuvre des représentants de l'État est extrêmement étroite. Car le gouvernement ne maîtrise pas la donne sur le terrain, qui reste volatile, même si l'armée israélienne et le Hezbollah se conforment globalement chacun à sa propre interprétation des termes de la trêve de dix jours.

Dans la banlieue sud de Beyrouth, le calme est revenu, mais rares sont ceux qui le croient durable. Les habitants rentrent surtout pour constater l'ampleur des dégâts ou mieux organiser leur séjour dans les lieux d'accueil où ils se sont réfugiés. Au sud du fleuve Litani, dont l'accès a été rouvert par l'armée libanaise, le sentiment de précarité est encore plus grand. L'accès à une cinquantaine de localités demeure impossible : dès l'entrée en vigueur de la trêve, l'armée israélienne a instauré une zone d'occupation frontalière profonde de 10 kilomètres dont les contours ont été officiellement publiés par le porte-parole de l'armée israélienne sur X. Démarquée par une « ligne jaune » - appellation calquée sur la zone tampon établie à Gaza en octobre dernier -, cette nouvelle occupation se traduit par de vastes opérations systématiques de dynamitages de bâtiments et d'infrastructures.

C'est dans ce cadre que l'armée israélienne aurait déploré des victimes. Deux militaires, dont un officier, sont « morts au combat » depuis vendredi, a annoncé Tsahal, précisant que 9 autres militaires ont été blessés lors de l'un des incidents. « Il ne s'agit pas d'une reprise des hostilités. Mais probablement de l'explosion au passage de patrouilles de mines ou de charges laissées par le Hezbollah » , explique-t-on de source informée . De fait, la formation alliée de l'Iran affiche officiellement sa volonté de respecter le cessez-le-feu et a démenti en conséquence son implication dans l'attaque qui a fait 4 victimes samedi, dont 1 mort, dans les rangs des Casques bleus français. Tout en imputant l'opération « délibérée » au Hezbollah, la France et la Force intérimaire des Nations unies (Finul) ont demandé une enquête.

Dans un discours, samedi soir, le secrétaire général du Parti de Dieu, Naïm Qassem, précise que son adhésion à la trêve reste conditionnelle et ne signifie en aucun cas un désengagement militaire. « Parce que nous ne faisons pas confiance à l'ennemi, les combattants de la résistance resteront sur le terrain, le doigt sur la gâchette » , a-t-il prévenu, insistant sur le fait qu' « il ne peut y avoir de cessez-le-feu imposé (au seul Hezbollah) ; il doit être mutuel » . Le Hezbollah a clairement fixé ses lignes rouges, explique l'analyste Hussein Ayoub : « pas question pour lui de revenir à la situation d'avant le 2 mars ». Naïm Qassem qualifie « d'humiliants » pour le Liban les termes de l'accord publié par le Département d'État, qui autorise exclusivement Israël à « prendre toutes les mesures nécessaires à sa légitime défense, à tout moment, contre toute attaque planifiée, imminente ou en cours ». Ce feu vert explicite désormais une entente israélo-américaine auparavant parallèle à l'accord de cessez-le-feu de novembre 2024 conclu avec le Liban. Les opérations quasi quotidiennes d'Israël ont fait plus de 400 morts en quinze mois. « Le Hezbollah ne reviendra pas à sa politique de «patience stratégique» en vertu de laquelle il ne ripostait pas », explique Nicholas Blanford, chercheur à l'Atlantic Council.

Le texte de l'accord de cessez-le-feu ne mentionne pas le respect de l'intégrité territoriale du Liban. Dans une adresse à la nation, le président de la République, Joseph Aoun, a cependant réaffirmé que l'obtention d'un retrait israélien du sud du Liban était sa priorité. « Je suis prêt à aller partout où il le faudra » pour atteindre cet objectif, a-t-il dit en réponse au Hezbollah, qui l'accuse de « concession gratuite » à Benyamin Netanyahou, auprès duquel il est invité à la Maison-Blanche. Le chef de l'État s'engage aussi à déployer l'armée libanaise sur tout le territoire national, ce qui suppose le désarmement du Hezbollah, « après » le retrait israélien.

Aussi incertaine que soit cette voie, elle est indispensable pour redonner aux autorités formelles le monopole de la négociation, estiment les soutiens du gouvernement. Les préparatifs des pourparlers sont en cours et rien n'a encore été annoncé quant à leur date ou leur format. Le premier ministre, Nawaf Salam, est attendu ce mardi au Luxembourg pour rencontrer les ministres des Affaires étrangères de l'Union européenne et il sera ensuite reçu à Paris par Emmanuel Macron. La France n'est pas présente officiellement dans les discussions bilatérales libano-israéliennes menées sous l'égide de Donald Trump.

Une dissymétrie qui conduit nombre d'analystes à s'inquiéter de la capacité de Beyrouth à faire prévaloir ses intérêts alors que rien ne garantit la pérennité du récent appel de Donald Trump à la « modération » israélienne au Liban. Le problème central tient à la configuration même des négociations, estime Hussein Ayoub. « Cette bilatéralisation affaiblit la position libanaise, alors que le véritable théâtre de négociation se situe dans un cadre régional élargi, où Beyrouth pourrait s'appuyer sur les soutiens croisés de Téhéran, d'Ankara ou de Riyad », dit-il. Dans tous les cas, la question libanaise ne peut être isolée du contexte régional, souligne Nicholas Blanford. « Si les négociations échouent complètement à Islamabad, on peut s'attendre à un effondrement du cessez-le-feu au Liban. En revanche, si un accord se dessine, il est possible que le Hezbollah ne réagisse pas à des violations mineures du cessez-le-feu par Israël », ce qui ouvrirait la voie à un processus libanais proprement dit.

Israel’s 78th Independence Day Special Coverage | An examination of battlefield complexity, identification failures, and the emerging role of thermal-based systems in improving force recognition and operational safety

Amir Haimovitch | 20/04/2026

The State of Israel is currently engaged in the longest war in its history, taking place simultaneously across several arenas: Iran, Gaza, Judea and Samaria, and southern Lebanon. The nature of the fighting, which combines maneuver warfare in dense urban terrain alongside covert, overt, and complex operations, presents a continuous challenge to the forces operating on the ground.

Within this reality, hundreds of incidents of friendly fire have occurred, in which dozens of soldiers were killed and many others were injured unintentionally. Some of the incidents ended without casualties, while others left a painful mark on the soldiers and the system as a whole.

For example, during an operational activity in Nablus, a force from the Duvdevan Unit operated alongside an observation and covering force under conditions of darkness and in a complex urban environment. During the operation, the covering force identified armed figures and opened fire under the assumption they were hiding terrorists; it later turned out that they were unit soldiers.

The incident ended without casualties, and luck was on their side. In another case, “luck” did not intervene: during a combined armor and paratroopers operation in Gaza, an armored force identified armed figures inside a building and fired several shells at them. Five paratroopers were killed and seven were wounded.

These events are not exceptional. They reflect the complexity of the modern battlefield, where many forces operate in close proximity, under limited visibility conditions and under high operational stress.

The battlefield is changing – and the challenge is intensifying

Friendly fire incidents usually result from a combination of factors: poor visibility conditions (night, smoke, weather), information overload, psychological pressure, and partial situational awareness. All of these, combined with human error and technological limitations, create conditions for misidentifying friendly forces as enemy forces.

The battlefield is often described as a “space of uncertainty,” and the ability to reduce this uncertainty is critical; however, it is clear that it cannot be eliminated entirely, especially in arenas where friendly and enemy forces operate in the same area.

The shift to multi-branch warfare, including infantry, armor, engineering, air, and additional forces, increases operational effectiveness, but at the same time sharpens the challenge of accurately and quickly identifying friendly forces in an environment saturated with threats.

In the past decade, there has been a significant technological leap: advanced command-and-control systems, thermal surveillance capabilities, drones, and airborne intelligence and strike systems have become standard, and firepower availability has increased significantly. Alongside these operational advantages, a sensor-rich and highly responsive battlefield has emerged, where any identification error can lead to fatal consequences.

Loss of the night vision advantage

Infrared night vision devices (image intensifiers) have become widely available and common in nearly every infantry squad and platoon – for example, monocular and binocular NVG systems used in night combat. However, this technology has also become widely available to the enemy. As a result, the use of marking devices (such as infrared lights) may expose forces with a high probability. Such markers no longer provide an adequate solution in the current reality and may even become a real threat when forces become visible and marked for the enemy.

Identification and distinction between forces in the field are no longer a supporting element, but a basic condition for situational awareness and operational safety. Soldiers operating at night, in dense urban terrain, or under fire require identification tools that enable clear distinction between friendly and enemy forces without reliance on continuous communication. The gap between battlefield complexity and identification capability creates a dangerous space in which decisions are made under uncertainty.

Thermal signaling systems – SpactralX’s Hotaru as a solution

Within this complex reality, advanced identification tools operating in the thermal domain have been developed. These systems are not widely available to the enemy, and certainly not mounted on their weapon systems, significantly reducing the probability of misidentifying friendly forces using them.

One such system is Hotaru, developed by the Israeli company SpectralX. It is a multispectral beacon mounted on a soldier’s helmet, enabling thermal signaling and identification.

The system is based on 360-degree thermal signaling technology, allowing ground and aerial support forces to identify the location of maneuvering units using thermal imaging systems. It provides an additional layer of certainty in the identification process. The system has two main advantages: reducing the time required to close fire loops through rapid identification of friendly forces, and lowering the risk of friendly fire through clear differentiation between friend and foe.

The Hotaru system was developed for the IDF following a procurement tender approximately four years ago, but with the outbreak of the Swords of Iron War and the rapid transition to combat, its full deployment in the forces was not completed. Most systems remain in storage due to a lack of training and structured doctrine.

In contrast, elite units such as Sayeret Shaldag, Unit 669, and Yamam (National Counter-Terrorism Unit) have successfully used it operationally. For example, during a rescue operation in Rafah in February 2024, the system was used to mark forces for supporting aircraft. It enabled precise identification under complex combat conditions and provided force protection with a high level of certainty regarding each unit’s position in urban terrain.

Today, defense establishments understand that such a capability cannot be introduced during combat alone. The decision is to integrate it already during training stages in military schools and basic training bases, so that soldiers arrive at their units with the system as part of their operational doctrine.

A complex battlefield

The implication is that friendly fire is a product of a complex battlefield, not merely human error. Alongside improvements in procedures, discipline, and doctrine, a technological solution is required to reduce real-time uncertainty. Infrared-based marking systems are not a viable long-term solution, as they may expose friendly forces.

In contrast, advanced thermal systems such as Hotaru, while not a standalone solution, can significantly reduce risk, improve situational awareness, and save the lives of dozens of soldiers. The life of a single soldier justifies the cost of such systems, and therefore there appears to be no real debate regarding the need for large-scale procurement of such thermal capabilities.

In an environment where life-or-death decisions are made within seconds, every element that clarifies the picture and distinguishes friend from foe can be the difference between harm and its prevention.

Amir Haimovitch is the CMO at SpectralX, a reserve Lieutenant Colonel, and aformer deputy commander of the Maglan reconnaissance unit.

Analysis | The Lebanon Playbook: What Israel Should Remember Now

Past campaigns against Hezbollah reveal enduring constraints on military power, and the strategic trade-offs between firepower, maneuver, and occupation

Ehud Eilam | 20/04/2026

The direct confrontation with Iran, especially given the distance, represents a new type of conflict for Israel. By contrast, the confrontation that has just (temporarily) ended with Hezbollah – namely, dealing with a guerrilla and terrorist organization in Lebanon – is a familiar challenge dating back to the 1970s. Israel must draw lessons from this experience, including the risks of relying on external actors, the difficulty of deploying forces in Lebanon, and the readiness of its ground forces, especially since fighting there may resume.

In 1978, during Operation Litani, the Israel Defense Forces seized territory in southern Lebanon and withdrew after roughly three months. That withdrawal was linked to the establishment of United Nations Interim Force in Lebanon (UNIFIL), whose mandate is set to expire this year – creating a kind of historical closure, as a “Litani II” scenario now appears to be unfolding. Israel is unlikely to accept another UN force in exchange for withdrawal; if it does agree to an international presence, it would prefer a more effective one, though that remains uncertain.

Following Operation Litani, Lebanese Christian militias led by Saad Haddad were strengthened as part of the struggle against the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO). Today, a deep reform of the Lebanese Army would be required, neutralizing its Shiite component and strengthening its Christian component, to enable it to restrain and disarm Hezbollah.

During the 1982 Lebanon War, the IDF advanced as far as Beirut and then gradually withdrew over the next 18 years. Key milestones included the 1985 redeployment to the security zone and the 2000 withdrawal from Lebanon. Today, a somewhat reversed process is underway: in 2024, the IDF established several positions inside Lebanon near the border, and in the current conflict additional areas in southern Lebanon were captured. If the ceasefire collapses, further territory may be seized.

Maintaining a permanent presence in these areas would require building and reinforcing fortifications, essentially well-protected outposts, to shield troops from Hezbollah fire. This will take time, and in the interim these positions may be targeted. In any case, supply routes– including those leading to the outposts – will remain vulnerable. This would effectively recreate the dynamics of the former security zone, though both sides are now stronger, making it a significant challenge for the IDF.

An alternative would be to avoid establishing additional outposts, or even reduce their number, and rely more on fire control and maneuver, including patrols and operations in southern Lebanon. However, such movements would be exposed to Hezbollah ambushes and fire. This reflects a longstanding debate: should defense rely on fixed positions or mobile operations? (Historically, one of the most notable IDF debates concerned the Bar-Lev Line along the Suez Canal.)

Another option is to conduct periodic, short-duration operations, similar to Operation Accountability (1993), Operation Grapes of Wrath (1996), the 1988 raid on the Hezbollah outpost in Maidoun, or earlier operations such as Operation Cauldron 2 in 1970. Airborne raids also remain a viable option, as in the past.

Israel’s ground forces entered the current campaign with substantial and relatively recent combat experience, primarily from the Gaza Strip, as ground operations in Lebanon in 2024 were relatively limited. However, the conditions in Lebanon differ significantly, especially in terms of terrain.

Lebanon spans only about 10,000 square kilometers but is narrow and elongated, providing Hezbollah with strategic depth. Since 1982, Israel’s ground forces have not conducted deep operations penetrating dozens of kilometers, required to reach Hezbollah strongholds in Beirut and the Bekaa Valley. Even then, uprooting Hezbollah would require a prolonged presence in those areas, with all the associated complications.

From the outset, Israel could have chosen a model similar to the 1993 or 1996 operations—relying primarily on aerial and artillery fire. However, following repeated rounds of conflict in Gaza up to 2023, which leaned heavily on firepower, Israel shifted its approach after October 7. Despite extensive use of airpower, a significant ground maneuver also took place in Lebanon, reflecting the understanding that firepower alone is insufficient.

The operations in 1993 and 1996 displaced large portions of the Lebanese population northward, aiming to pressure the government to restrain Hezbollah. Today, many residents of southern Lebanon have again fled north. Yet, as in the 1990s, Lebanon’s structural conditions – a weak government and a strong Hezbollah – continue to hinder, and may ultimately prevent, any meaningful change. Israel and the Lebanese government may reach an agreement or general understandingsת some of them vagueת without addressing the core issue posed by Hezbollah.

In 1978, Operation Litani involved two reduced divisions. In 1982, roughly six divisions operated in Lebanon, reaching Beirut—about 90 kilometers from the border. In the current conflict, around five divisions operated in southern Lebanon, though not at full strength. Even so, overcrowding forces in a relatively small, fragmented, mountainous, and forested area is unwise – a clear lesson from 1982.

After two and a half years of sustained operations and multiple missions, Israel’s ground forces require rest, reorganization, and reinforcement. Israel should therefore avoid becoming entrenched in a prolonged conflict in Lebanon – especially if forces are also engaged in the West Bank, the Gaza Strip, or Syria, where large-scale and prolonged confrontations could erupt at any time.

In the bottom line, Israel must internalize the lessons accumulated over decades of confronting guerrilla warfare and terrorism in southern Lebanon. This is particularly true regarding constraints, the integration of firepower and maneuver, the costs involved, and the challenges of maintaining a sustained presence on Lebanese soil.

The talks between the United States and Iran mediated by Pakistan have not failed. They do face significant challenges.

The recent statements by US President Donald Trump and the shuttle diplomacy carried out by senior Pakistani military and government leaders in regional capitals have raised hopes of ending the US-Israel war with Iran through negotiations. This could initially take the form of a “framework agreement” between the United States and Iran to lay the groundwork for a final deal.

Speaking to reporters at the White House on Thursday, Trump struck an optimistic tone, saying the war was “about to end”, as “almost all” issues had been resolved. The remaining differences would be addressed in talks that he said would resume soon. He also said he might travel to Islamabad if a final agreement were signed there.

Despite Trump’s tendency to make exaggerated statements, his remarks this time appeared to be backed by a series of intensive diplomatic activities in the region. The surprise visit by Pakistan’s army chief, Field Marshal Asim Munir, to Tehran for talks with Iranian officials—apparently to convey messages from Washington—suggested that the ground was being prepared for another round of negotiations between the US and Iran.

The messages conveyed were intended to address differences between the two sides over the remaining issues, as well as to discuss efforts aimed at securing a ceasefire in Lebanon.

At the same time, Pakistan’s Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif embarked on a three-country tour—Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Turkiye—to brief their leaders on the latest developments in the talks aimed at ending the war.

This also indicates that diplomatic activity was in full swing in preparation for another round of direct talks between Washington and Tehran.

With the ceasefire among the US, Israel, and Iran holding since April 8, the announcement of a 10-day truce between Lebanon and Israel has boosted optimism and was widely seen as a step towards a peace agreement between Washington and Tehran.

Iran welcomed the truce, which received global backing. Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei said Tehran views the truce as part of a broader understanding with Washington reached during talks mediated by Pakistan.

In fact, controversy arose shortly after the ceasefire between the US and Iran was announced, when Iran and Pakistan said it included a truce in Lebanon as part of a broader regional ceasefire, but Trump denied that.

This required talks among Israel, the US, and Lebanon, which culminated in the ceasefire in Lebanon. In response, Iran announced it would allow all commercial ships to pass through the Strait of Hormuz for the remainder of the temporary truce period—before matters became somewhat complicated.

All of this came after negotiations held between the US and Iran in Islamabad on April 12.

These were the highest-level direct talks between them in more than four decades, during which there were no diplomatic relations between the two countries. The dispatch by both sides of high-level delegations indicated their seriousness about finding a way out of the conflict.

Many international media outlets rushed to declare that the talks were inconclusive and ended in failure, as if an agreement on such thorny issues could be reached within just a few hours.

In reality, the Islamabad talks were neither a major achievement nor a failure; the two delegations returned to their capitals to consult with their leaderships in a generally positive atmosphere, and neither side said the talks had collapsed.

The diplomatic option remained on the table for both sides, keeping the door open to the possibility of continuing negotiations. Diplomatic engagement continued through Pakistan, which stepped up its efforts to persuade the two parties to show flexibility and maintain back-channel communications in order to narrow the gaps in their positions.

The Islamabad talks revealed how far apart the two sides’ positions were, as reflected in the 15-point plan put forward by the US and the 10-point proposal presented by Iran.

Tehran’s core demands included guarantees that there would be no future American or Israeli attacks on Iran and its regional allies, the lifting of sanctions, the unfreezing of assets, international recognition of its right to uranium enrichment, and the continuation of its control over the Strait of Hormuz.

The US’s demands included strict Iranian commitments not to pursue nuclear weapons, insistence that Tehran carry out no enrichment, removing Iran’s stockpile of highly enriched uranium from the country, and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz.

When the talks ended, the US side claimed that Iran had not responded to its nuclear concerns, while Iran asserted that the American negotiators had made unrealistic demands.

But both sides acknowledged that progress had been made, despite key issues remaining unresolved, including the future status of the Strait of Hormuz. The US proposed sharing the revenue from customs fees for the strait, but Iran rejected the idea.

Subsequent indirect contacts sought to address contentious points on the nuclear issue and the strategic waterway, as Pakistani mediators urged both sides to be more flexible.

These issues are expected to dominate the talks in a second round if it is held, as Pakistani mediators have privately said they made progress on the “contentious issues”, although Iranian officials have expressed a more cautious stance.

The main difference that must be resolved concerns the nuclear issue: the US proposes that Iran carry out no uranium enrichment for 20 years, which it believes would ensure that Tehran does not pursue a nuclear weapons programme.

Iran has repeatedly stressed that it will not build a nuclear bomb, but that it has the right to enrichment for peaceful purposes under the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, to which it is a party. Mohammad Eslami, head of the Atomic Energy Organization of Iran, has stated that the talks must recognise Iran’s rights, interests, and dignity if they are to bear fruit.

The question is whether the US would agree to enrichment at less than three percent —far below weapons-grade levels—for five years, as Iran reportedly offered.

As for the other issue related to removing nuclear material, it can likely be addressed through Tehran’s offer to dilute the concentration of its 400kg stockpile of highly enriched uranium to the lowest possible level inside Iran, while granting the International Atomic Energy Agency full access to verify it.
